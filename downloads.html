MendozaH_Thesis Manuscript FINAL SIGNED.pdf

For my undergraduate thesis, I studied the effects of urbanization on stream ecosystems by analyzing benthic macroinvertebrates from different waterways around UP Diliman. Instead of relying solely on taxonomic identification, I additionally used functional traits—like feeding type, respiration mode, reproduction, and movement—to get a deeper understanding of how these organisms respond to pollution and environmental stress. I assessed over 18,000 specimens up to the genera level across different land use types and seasons, and gained experience thru everything from research design, towards fieldwork, lab protocol, to data wrangling using QGIS, RStudio, and PAST. Results showed significantly different macroinvertebrate communities between wet and dry seasons, with higher diversity and tolerance scores in the dry season. Functional traits, however, stayed fairly consistent, suggesting a uniformly stressed aquatic environment. My study combined elements of limnology, entomology, and invertebrate zoology, and allowed me to apply and demonstrate research and technical skills I’d built since high school.
